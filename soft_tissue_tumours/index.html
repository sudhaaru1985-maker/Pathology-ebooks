Clinical Trigger

A 58-year-old male presents to the surgical OPD with a 3-month history of a slowly enlarging, painless mass in the posterior thigh. He initially ignored it, thinking it was a "pulled muscle." On examination, the mass is firm, deep-seated, approximately 7 cm, and non-tender. There is no overlying skin change. His past medical history includes chemotherapy for colon cancer 10 years ago.

Ultrasound-guided biopsy shows spindle cells in a herringbone pattern with areas of necrosis.

→ As you study this module, consider: What is the likely diagnosis? What are the differential diagnoses? What prognostic factors apply to this patient? How would you distinguish this from a benign process?

⚠ Diagnostic Delay is Common
Soft tissue sarcomas are frequently misdiagnosed as haematomas or "pulled muscles." Any soft tissue mass >5 cm, deep to fascia, or persisting beyond 4–6 weeks warrants imaging and biopsy.

05a

Fatty Tumours

The most common benign soft tissue tumour. Arises from mature adipocytes.

ClinicalSoft, mobile, lobulated lump — usually painless. Commonly subcutaneous.
GrossWell-encapsulated, yellowish mass with smooth, greasy surface.
HistologyEncapsulated mass of benign-looking mature adipocytes — indistinguishable from normal fat.
TreatmentSimple excision. Recurrence is rare.
Memory hook: Lipoma = fat with a capsule, normal-looking cells, soft and mobile = benign.

Malignant tumour of adipose tissue. Second most common soft tissue sarcoma in adults.

Age/SiteAdults 50–60 years. Deep soft tissues & retroperitoneum.
Diagnostic CellLipoblast — multivacuolated cell that scallops the nucleus (pathognomonic).
SubtypesWell-differentiated, Myxoid (most common), Pleomorphic (worst prognosis).
GeneticsMyxoid → t(12;16) FUS-DDIT3. Well-diff → MDM2/CDK4 amplification.

Subtypes Comparison

SubtypeDifferentiationPrognosisKey Feature
Well-differentiatedHigh (resembles fat)Good (low grade)MDM2/CDK4 amplification
MyxoidIntermediateIntermediateChicken-wire vasculature; t(12;16)
PleomorphicLow (anaplastic)Poor (high grade)Bizarre giant cells
Exam Trap: Lipoblasts are REQUIRED for diagnosis of liposarcoma. Lipoma never contains lipoblasts.
05b

Fibrous Tumours & Tumour-Like Lesions

Key Concept — Reactive Pseudosarcomatous Proliferations: These are non-neoplastic lesions that form tumour-like masses in response to trauma. They mimic sarcoma clinically and histologically. Include Nodular Fasciitis and Myositis Ossificans.

The most common pseudosarcoma. Non-neoplastic reactive fibroblastic proliferation often related to local trauma.

PopulationCommonly affects adults (any age). Rapid growth over weeks.
SitesForearm, chest wall, and back.
ClinicalRapidly growing, possibly painful mass — alarming but BENIGN.
HistologyHighly cellular with immature fibroblasts arranged randomly. Abundant mitotic figures (but atypical mitoses absent).
TreatmentSimple excision. Spontaneous regression can occur.
Classic Trap: Abundant mitotic figures → students may call it malignant. Remember: mitoses can be abundant in reactive conditions. Lack of ATYPICAL mitoses is reassuring.

Characterised by metaplastic bone formation in muscle following trauma.

PopulationAthletic adolescents and young adults post-trauma.
SiteMuscles of extremities.
EvolutionEarly: painful, swollen. Late: painless, hard mass (mineralized & ossified).
Key FeaturePeripheral mineralisation on X-ray (the lesion mineralizes from outside in — opposite of osteosarcoma).
Zonal Pattern: Centre = undifferentiated cells → Middle = osteoblasts → Periphery = mature bone. Osteosarcoma is the OPPOSITE (central ossification).

Large, infiltrative fibroblastic masses that are locally aggressive but do not metastasise. Can recur after incomplete excision.

AgeAny age; more common in teens & young adults.
SitesExtra-abdominal (shoulders, chest wall, thigh), Abdominal wall (females), Intra-abdominal (mesentery).
AssociationFAP (Familial Adenomatous Polyposis) → APC gene mutation. Sporadic → CTNNB1 (β-catenin) mutation.
HistologyBland fibrocytic cells in storiform/haphazard pattern with abundant collagen. Low mitotic rate.
Remember: Desmoid = no metastasis but locally destructive. High recurrence rate after incomplete excision (up to 70%).

Aggressive malignant tumour of fibroblasts. Can occur anywhere in the body.

GrossUnencapsulated, infiltrative mass with fish-flesh appearance. Areas of haemorrhage and necrosis.
HistologyHighly cellular with spindle cells in classic herringbone pattern.
PrognosisRecurrence in 50% of cases. Metastasis in >25% cases.
PatternHerringbone = fibrosarcoma (see architectural patterns)
05c

Skeletal Muscle Tumours

Most common soft tissue sarcoma of childhood and adolescence.

SitesHead & neck, genitourinary tract, extremities.
Diagnostic CellRhabdomyoblast — eosinophilic granular cytoplasm. Elongated forms = strap cells / tadpole cells.
IHC MarkersDesmin MYOD1 Myogenin
SubtypesEmbryonal (most common), Alveolar (worst prognosis), Pleomorphic (adults).

Embryonal RMS / Sarcoma Botyroides

  • Most common subtype of RMS.
  • Sarcoma botyroides = grape-like polypoid mass projecting into body cavities (nasopharynx, bladder, vagina).
  • Key histological feature: cambium layer (dense condensation of tumour cells beneath epithelium) + cytoplasmic cross-striations.
  • Genetics: loss of heterozygosity at 11p15.

Alveolar RMS

  • Worst prognosis among RMS subtypes.
  • Tumour cells arranged in alveolar-like spaces separated by fibrous septa.
  • Genetics: t(2;13) → PAX3-FOXO1A or t(1;13) → PAX7-FOXO1A.
Exam Trap: RMS is the most common soft tissue SARCOMA in CHILDREN — not adults. In adults, the most common is liposarcoma/MFH (undifferentiated pleomorphic sarcoma).

Despite the name, less than 10% occur intra-articularly. Mostly in deep soft tissue around knee/thigh in young adults.

Age Group20–40 years.
SiteDeep soft tissues near knee and thigh. NOT truly synovial origin.
HistologyBiphasic: epithelial gland-like structures + spindle cells. Monophasic: only one type.
Geneticst(X;18) → SYT-SSX fusion.
IHCCytokeratin EMA (epithelial markers in a "non-epithelial" tumour).
Classic Trap: Synovial sarcoma stains positive for CYTOKERATIN despite being a soft tissue sarcoma — not a carcinoma!
05d

Peripheral Nerve Tumours

Associated with familial syndromes: NF1 (neurofibromas), NF2 (bilateral vestibular schwannomas), and Schwannomatosis.

Benign tumour of Schwann cell differentiation. Arises from peripheral nerves but does not invade the nerve.

SiteVestibular branch of VIII CN at cerebellopontine angle (acoustic neuroma), peripheral nerves.
GeneticsMutation of NF2 gene on chromosome 22 (merlin protein).
GrossWell-circumscribed, encapsulated, firm grey mass attached to — but not invading — the nerve.
IHCS-100 protein strongly positive.

Two Histological Patterns — Antoni A & Antoni B

Antoni A Densely cellular. Spindle cells in fascicles. Nuclei aligned in parallel rows with nuclear-free zones = Verocay bodies (palisading pattern).
Antoni B Loose, hypocellular. Myxoid extracellular matrix. Less organised architecture.
Memory: Antoni A = Arranged (orderly palisading, Verocay bodies). Antoni B = Blurred (loose, disorganised).

Benign tumour where neoplastic Schwann cells are admixed with fibroblasts, mast cells, and CD34+ spindle cells — unlike schwannoma which is a pure Schwann cell tumour.

GeneticsAssociated with NF1 (chr 17).
TypesCutaneous (pedunculated), Diffuse (plaque-like, NF1), Plexiform (deep, pathognomonic of NF1).
HistologyUnencapsulated nodule in dermis/subcutis. Mixed cells in myxoid stroma.
Malignant RiskPlexiform neurofibromas carry risk of transformation to MPNST.
Schwannoma vs Neurofibroma: Schwannoma = pure Schwann cells, encapsulated, NF2. Neurofibroma = mixed cells, unencapsulated, NF1. Plexiform NF = pathognomonic of NF1.

Aggressive spindle cell sarcoma arising from peripheral nerves or from pre-existing neurofibromas.

Risk Factors50% arise in NF1 patients. Can arise de novo or from plexiform neurofibroma.
HistologyHigh-grade spindle cell sarcoma. Loss of S-100 expression (unlike benign counterparts).
PrognosisPoor. 5-year survival ~20–50% depending on grade and resectability.
IHCFocal/lost S-100. Positive SOX10 may help.

A 6-year-old boy is brought to the clinic with a grape-like mass protruding from the urethral meatus. Biopsy reveals primitive round cells with eosinophilic cytoplasm and a dense condensation of cells beneath the epithelial surface (cambium layer). IHC is positive for desmin and myogenin.

What is the diagnosis?

  • BEmbryonal rhabdomyosarcoma — sarcoma botyroides subtype

  • DEwing sarcoma
Same patient. Genetic analysis is performed.

Step 2 of 2 — Genetics

Which genetic finding would indicate the WORST prognosis in rhabdomyosarcoma?

  • ALoss of heterozygosity at 11p15
  • Bt(2;13) producing PAX3-FOXO1A fusion
  • CMYOD1 point mutation
  • DCDK4 amplification

Case 3 — Young Adult with Nerve Pain

A 28-year-old woman presents with multiple soft pedunculated skin nodules over her trunk and upper limbs since childhood. She also has café-au-lait spots. Biopsy of one nodule shows unencapsulated lesion composed of Schwann cells, fibroblasts, mast cells and CD34+ spindle cells in a myxoid stroma.

Single Best Answer — Diagnosis and Implications

What is the diagnosis and what is the greatest oncological concern for this patient?

  • AMultiple schwannomas (NF2); risk of bilateral acoustic neuromas
  • BMultiple lipomas; cosmetic concern only
  • CCutaneous neurofibromas (NF1); risk of malignant transformation to MPNST
  • DCutaneous desmoid tumours; local recurrence risk

Key Take-Home Messages

01Soft tissue tumours are classified by tissue of origin. Each tissue has a benign and a malignant counterpart.
02Lipoblast = diagnostic cell of liposarcoma. A multivacuolated cell that scallops the nucleus. Never found in lipoma.
03Nodular fasciitis = benign pseudosarcoma. Abundant mitotic figures ≠ malignant; absence of ATYPICAL mitoses is key.
04Desmoid tumours = locally aggressive, no metastasis, but HIGH recurrence. Associated with APC/β-catenin mutations.
05Herringbone pattern = Fibrosarcoma. Storiform = Fibrohistiocytic. Palisading + Verocay = Schwannoma.
06RMS is the most common soft tissue SARCOMA in CHILDREN. Cambium layer + botyroides pattern = embryonal subtype. PAX3-FOXO1A = alveolar (worst prognosis).
07Synovial sarcoma is NOT from synovium. It stains for cytokeratin/EMA (like a carcinoma) and has t(X;18).
08Schwannoma (NF2, encapsulated, Antoni A+B, pure S-100+) vs Neurofibroma (NF1, unencapsulated, mixed cells). Plexiform neurofibroma = pathognomonic of NF1 and carries MPNST risk.
09Adverse prognostic factors: tumour size >5 cm, high histological grade, deep location, positive surgical margins, age >50, recurrent disease.
10MRI is the investigation of choice for extremity soft tissue masses. CT for abdominal/retroperitoneal lesions.

Exam Focus — Common Traps & Rapid Recall

⚠ Common Exam Traps

  • Mitotic figures in nodular fasciitis → NOT malignant; look for ATYPICAL mitoses
  • Synovial sarcoma stains cytokeratin/EMA → NOT a carcinoma
  • Lipoma ≠ Liposarcoma: lipoblasts are REQUIRED for liposarcoma diagnosis
  • Schwannoma (NF2, encapsulated) ≠ Neurofibroma (NF1, unencapsulated)
  • RMS is the #1 soft tissue sarcoma in CHILDREN — not adults (adults = liposarcoma/MFH)
  • Desmoid = NO metastasis but locally destructive + high recurrence
  • Myositis ossificans mineralizes PERIPHERALLY — opposite of osteosarcoma
  • S-100 loss in MPNST distinguishes it from benign nerve sheath tumours
  • "Synovial" sarcoma rarely arises from synovium — misleading name!
  • Sarcoma botyroides = subtype of embryonal RMS, NOT a separate entity

→ Rapid Recall Associations

  • Lipoblast → Liposarcoma
  • Cambium layer + Botyroides → Embryonal RMS
  • Verocay bodies + Antoni A/B → Schwannoma
  • Herringbone → Fibrosarcoma
  • Storiform → MFH / Fibrohistiocytic tumours
  • t(X;18) SYT-SSX → Synovial sarcoma
  • t(2;13) PAX3-FOXO1A → Alveolar RMS
  • HHV-8 → Kaposi sarcoma
  • NF1 → Neurofibroma / MPNST risk
  • NF2 → Schwannoma (bilateral acoustic)
  • APC / CTNNB1 → Desmoid (fibromatosis)
  • COL1A1-PDGFB t(17;22) → DFSP
  • MDM2, CDK4 amplification → Well-diff liposarcoma
  • Desmin + Myogenin → RMS
  • CD31 + vWF → Angiosarcoma / Endothelial
