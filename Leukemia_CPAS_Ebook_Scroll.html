Prior knowledge bridge

Connecting to what you already know

You have already studied normal haematopoiesis — the process by which a pluripotent stem cell differentiates along two main pathways:

Myeloid pathway (CFU-GEMM)

Myeloblast → Promyelocyte → Myelocyte → Metamyelocyte → Band → Neutrophil
Also gives rise to: erythrocytes, monocytes, megakaryocytes/platelets

Lymphoid pathway (CFU-L)

Lymphoid stem cell → Lymphoblast → Prolymphocyte → Lymphocyte → Plasma cell
B cells, T cells, and NK cells all arise from this lineage

The leukemia connection
Leukemia occurs when a genetic mutation "freezes" a precursor cell at an immature stage, preventing normal differentiation. The frozen cell continues to proliferate, filling the bone marrow with non-functional cells — physically crowding out all normal cell lines. This single mechanism explains almost all clinical features you will encounter.

Patient presentation

Ahmad, a 7-year-old boy, is brought to the emergency department. He has been pale and lethargic for three weeks, has had two episodes of nosebleeds this month, and has a persistent fever. On examination: cervical lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, and petechiae over the lower limbs.

Think before you read on — what single pathological process could simultaneously explain the very high WBC, the low Hb, the very low platelets, and the recurrent infections?
The answer
Marrow replacement by a leukemic clone. When malignant cells fill the bone marrow they crowd out normal precursors — reducing erythrocytes (→ anaemia), platelets (→ bleeding), and functional neutrophils (→ infections) simultaneously. The high WBC consists almost entirely of non-functional blast cells.

Individual leukemias

Tap each to expand

Definition
Malignant neoplastic proliferation and accumulation of immature, nonfunctional myeloid blast cells in the bone marrow. Most common acute leukaemia in adults. Rapidly fatal within weeks to months if untreated.

Pathogenesis

Mutations in genes encoding transcription factors for myeloid differentiation → differentiation arrest → myeloblasts accumulate → normal haematopoiesis is crowded out.

Key genetic event — APML (AML-M3)
Translocation t(15;17) → PML-RARα fusion gene → differentiation arrested at promyelocyte stage. Abnormal promyelocytes contain procoagulant granules → their release triggers DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation).

Clinical features (from marrow failure)

  • Fever & recurrent infections — functional neutropenia (blasts non-functional)
  • Anaemia — erythroid precursors displaced
  • Bleeding, petechiae, purpura — thrombocytopenia
  • Bone pain, sternal tenderness — marrow infiltration and expansion
  • Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly — organ infiltration by blasts
  • Gingival hyperplasia — characteristic of monocytic subtypes (M4/M5)

Laboratory & morphology

  • WBC: <1 × 10⁹/L to >100 × 10⁹/L; >20% myeloblasts (WHO threshold)
  • Normocytic normochromic anaemia; reduced reticulocyte count
  • BM: hypercellular; near-complete effacement by blasts
  • Myeloblasts: large cells, granular cytoplasm, fine lacy chromatin, prominent nucleoli
  • Auer rods: pink rod-like cytoplasmic inclusions — pathognomonic for AML
  • MPO (myeloperoxidase) stain: positive
AML — myeloblast
  • Large blast
  • Granular cytoplasm
  • Fine lacy chromatin
  • Prominent nucleoli
  • Auer rods present
  • MPO positive
ALL — lymphoblast (comparison)
  • Small blast
  • Scant cytoplasm
  • Dense chromatin
  • Indistinct nucleoli
  • No Auer rods
  • TdT+, PAS dot positive

Classification

FAB (M0–M7) — based on morphology: M0 Undifferentiated · M1 minimal maturation · M2 with maturation · M3 APML t(15;17) · M4 myelomonocytic · M4eos with eosinophilia · M5 monocytic · M6 erythroid · M7 megakaryocytic

WHO 2016 — incorporates cytogenetics and molecular features. Reduced diagnostic blast threshold from 30% (FAB) to ≥20%.

Complications

  • DIC — especially APML (M3); life-threatening haemorrhage
  • Tumour lysis syndrome — massive cell death releases intracellular contents
  • Leukostasis — very high WBC causes vascular sludging
Definition
Malignant transformation of an early haematopoietic progenitor. Abnormal proliferation of progenitor AND mature myeloid cells. Unlike AML, neoplastic cells retain terminal differentiation capacity — producing the full myeloid spectrum.

Pathogenesis — The Philadelphia chromosome

Reciprocal translocation t(9;22) → Philadelphia chromosome → BCR-ABL1 fusion gene → constitutively active tyrosine kinase → uncontrolled myeloid proliferation.

Targeted therapy
Imatinib — specifically inhibits BCR-ABL1 tyrosine kinase. First successful molecularly targeted cancer therapy. Transformed CML survival from 3–5 years to near-normal life expectancy.

Clinical features

  • Elderly age group; massive splenomegaly (extramedullary haematopoiesis)
  • Blood film: full myeloid spectrum; prominent basophilia
  • Low LAP score — neoplastic neutrophils lack leukocyte alkaline phosphatase

Definition
Malignant neoplastic proliferation and accumulation of immature, nonfunctional lymphoid blast cells in the bone marrow. Most common cancer in children; peak age 2–10 years. Excellent prognosis with treatment (>90% cure in children).

Etiology

  • Genetic/hereditary: Down syndrome, Fanconi anaemia, Bloom syndrome, ataxia-telangiectasia, Kostmann syndrome
  • Radiation: High-dose exposure (e.g. atomic bomb survivors)
  • Chemical/occupational: Benzene, petroleum products, paint, embalming fluids, ethylene oxide
  • Drugs: Alkylating agents, topoisomerase II inhibitors, chloramphenicol, phenylbutazone, chloroquine, methoxypsoralen

Pathophysiology

  1. Genetic mutations "freeze" the lymphoid precursor in its immature state — differentiation arrest
  2. Additional mutations disrupting proliferation control → uncontrolled clonal expansion of lymphoblasts
  3. Leukemic clone displaces normal BM cells → neutropenia, anaemia, thrombocytopenia
  4. Clinical symptoms arise from these deficiencies — not from the blasts themselves

Clinical features

  • Fever, repeated infections; anaemia; bleeding; bone pain, sternal tenderness; lymphadenopathy; hepatosplenomegaly

Laboratory findings

  • WBC: usually raised but may be normal or decreased; >20% lymphoblasts; neutropenia; thrombocytopenia
  • BM: hypercellular; >20% lymphoblasts
  • Hyperuricaemia; possible renal failure; increased CSF lymphoblasts

Morphology and special stains

  • PAS stain: dot positivity in ALL blasts — negative in AML
  • TdT: positive in ALL — negative in AML
  • Immunophenotyping (CD markers): identifies B-cell or T-cell lineage — essential for treatment stratification

FAB classification

  • L1 (85% children): Homogeneous; small blasts, regular nucleus, scant cytoplasm
  • L2 (70% adults): Varied; irregular nucleus, heterogeneous chromatin, large nucleoli
  • L3 Burkitt's (<2%): Large blasts, prominent nucleoli, abundant vacuolated cytoplasm
Important — t(9;22) in ALL
Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22)/BCR-ABL1 can occur in ALL (especially adult ALL) and carries a poor prognosis. This is distinct from CML where t(9;22) is the defining mutation. Context (myeloid vs lymphoid, chronic vs acute) determines its significance.
Definition
Chronic neoplastic disorder of mature small lymphocytes. Most common leukaemia in adults; predominantly elderly. Indolent — often found incidentally on routine FBC.

CLL vs SLL: same disease — lymphocyte count >4000/μL = CLL (blood/BM); <4000/μL = SLL (tissue).

Pathogenesis

1. Inhibition of apoptosis (key mechanism — NOT uncontrolled proliferation):

  • Tumour cells upregulate BCL2 protein → inhibits programmed cell death
  • Lymphocytes accumulate progressively over years — indolent course

2. Immune dysregulation:

  • Suppresses normal B-cell function → hypogammaglobulinaemia → infections
  • Autoantibodies → autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, autoimmune thrombocytopenia

3. Bone marrow effacement: tumour cells gradually replace normal BM cells → eventual cytopenias

Morphology

Peripheral blood: increased mature small lymphocytes; smudge cells (Gumprecht shadows) — CLL lymphocytes are fragile; they rupture during smear preparation — pathognomonic for CLL.

Lymph node: sheets of small lymphocytes efface architecture; proliferation centres — foci of mitotically active larger cells — pathognomonic for CLL/SLL.

Immunophenotyping

Mature B-cell markers: CD19, CD20, CD23, surface immunoglobulin. Also expresses CD5 — a T-cell marker aberrantly expressed on neoplastic B-cells.

Clinical features

  • Often asymptomatic — incidental finding
  • Lymphadenopathy and hepatosplenomegaly
  • Complications: infections; autoimmune haemolytic anaemia; autoimmune thrombocytopenia
Overview
Myeloproliferative disorders share a common mechanism: activating mutations in tyrosine kinase genes drive constitutive proliferative signals. Unlike AML, neoplastic cells retain terminal differentiation capacity — producing excessive mature formed elements. Key mutations: JAK2 V617F (PV, ET, myelofibrosis) and BCR-ABL1 (CML).

Polycythemia vera (PV)

  • Panmyelosis (erythroid + granulocytic + megakaryocytic proliferation); dominated by excess red cell mass
  • JAK2 V617F mutation in >95%; JAK2 exon 12 in 3%
  • Low serum erythropoietin — distinguishes from secondary/reactive polycythaemia
  • WHO 2016: elevated Hb/haematocrit/red cell mass + BM panmyelosis + JAK2 mutation; minor: low EPO
  • Clinical: thromboses and infarctions (increased viscosity); plethora; extramedullary haematopoiesis; GI haemorrhage

Essential thrombocythemia (ET)

  • Rare (1.5 per 100,000); proliferation of megakaryocytes → marked increase in platelets (>1 million/μL)
  • Morphologically abnormal giant platelets; splenomegaly; mucosal haemorrhage; thromboses

Primary myelofibrosis

  • Characterised by bone marrow fibrosis; may follow CML, PV, or ET
  • Peripheral smear — leukoerythroblastosis: teardrop cells (dacrocytes), nucleated erythroid precursors, immature WBCs, large abnormal platelets
  • Advanced: BM hypocellular and fibrotic → pancytopenia; massive splenomegaly from extramedullary haematopoiesis

Application cases

Guided clinical vignettes

Case 1 — Acute myeloid presentation

Mr. Hafiz, 52 years old, presents with a 3-week history of fatigue, recurrent fevers, and easy bruising. He reports bleeding gums for 2 weeks. On examination: pallor, gingival hyperplasia, petechiae and purpura, hepatomegaly.

WBC 78 × 10⁹/L
Hb 79 g/L
Plt 18 × 10⁹/L
Blasts >20% with Auer rods

BM biopsy: 85% blasts, hypercellular with near-complete effacement of normal haematopoiesis.

Step 1 — Interpret the paradox
Mr. Hafiz has WBC 78 × 10⁹/L yet presents with recurrent fevers and signs of infection. Which mechanism best explains this clinical paradox?
AMyeloblasts release cytokines that suppress normal neutrophil function
BHigh WBC causes hyperviscosity, impairing neutrophil delivery to tissues
CMyeloblasts crowd out normal myeloid precursors in the marrow, causing functional neutropenia despite a high total WBC
DBlast infiltration of lymph nodes prevents T-cell mediated immunity
Correct
This is the key paradox of acute leukaemia. The elevated WBC consists almost entirely of non-functional myeloblasts. Normal neutrophil precursors are physically displaced from the marrow — producing severe functional neutropenia despite an extremely high total WBC. The patient is immunocompromised even with a very high WBC.
Not quite
The correct mechanism is myelophthisis — physical displacement of normal marrow precursors by the leukemic clone. This causes functional neutropenia (too few normal, working neutrophils) despite a very high total WBC that consists almost entirely of non-functional blasts.
Step 2 — Identify the pathognomonic finding
The blood film shows myeloblasts with Auer rods. What are Auer rods and what is their diagnostic significance?
ASmall nuclear inclusions found in lymphoblasts; confirm ALL diagnosis
BPink rod-like cytoplasmic inclusions from condensed granules; pathognomonic for AML — never found in ALL
CCytoplasmic vacuoles seen in Burkitt's leukaemia; indicate ALL-L3 subtype
DFragmented nuclear remnants from cell death; seen in any acute leukaemia
Correct
Auer rods are pink rod-like cytoplasmic inclusions formed from fused azurophilic granules — pathognomonic for AML, never found in ALL. Their presence immediately confirms a myeloid blast population. This distinction is critical because AML and ALL require completely different treatment regimens.
Not quite
Auer rods are cytoplasmic (not nuclear) inclusions specific to myeloblasts in AML. They are formed from fused azurophilic granules and appear as pink rod-like structures. They are never seen in ALL — making them one of the most diagnostically useful findings on a blood film.
Step 3 — Identify the complication
Mr. Hafiz's clotting screen: PT prolonged, APTT prolonged, fibrinogen very low, D-dimer markedly elevated. Which AML subtype and mechanism explains this?
AAML-M2 with maturation; bone marrow necrosis releases procoagulants
BAML-M3 (APML); abnormal promyelocytes with t(15;17) release procoagulant granules, triggering DIC
CAML-M5 monocytic; monocytes produce excess tissue factor throughout the body
DAML-M7 megakaryocytic; excess platelets activate the coagulation cascade
Correct
DIC is the hallmark complication of AML-M3 (APML), caused by t(15;17)/PML-RARα. Differentiation is arrested at the promyelocyte stage — abnormal promyelocytes accumulate procoagulant granules that trigger systemic DIC when released. Clinically urgent: APML is treated with ATRA (all-trans retinoic acid), fundamentally different from other AML subtypes.
Not quite
DIC in AML specifically indicates AML-M3 (APML) due to t(15;17)/PML-RARα. The differentiation arrest at the promyelocyte stage results in cells packed with procoagulant granules — their release triggers systemic coagulation. Recognising APML is urgent because it requires ATRA, not standard chemotherapy.
Case 2 — Chronic lymphoid presentation

Puan Rohani, 68 years old, attends her annual health screening. She feels entirely well with no complaints.

WBC 54 × 10⁹/L
Hb 112 g/L
Plt 138 × 10⁹/L

Blood film: numerous small mature lymphocytes and smudge cells. Mild cervical lymphadenopathy and splenomegaly. Immunophenotyping: CD19+, CD20+, CD23+, CD5+.

Step 4 — Diagnose and explain
Puan Rohani is completely asymptomatic despite a WBC of 54 × 10⁹/L. Which diagnosis and underlying mechanism best explains her presentation?
AALL — differentiation arrest of lymphoid precursors causing blast accumulation and marrow failure
BCML — BCR-ABL1 driven myeloid proliferation producing immature cells
CCLL — BCL2 upregulation inhibiting apoptosis causes accumulation of long-lived mature B-lymphocytes over years
DPolycythemia vera — JAK2 mutation causing excess lymphocyte production
Correct
CLL is driven primarily by inhibition of apoptosis, not uncontrolled proliferation. BCL2 upregulation blocks programmed cell death, allowing mature but immunologically incompetent B-lymphocytes to accumulate gradually over years. The CD5+/CD19+/CD20+/CD23+ profile and smudge cells confirm CLL.
Not quite
The key features — elderly patient, incidental finding, mature lymphocytes, smudge cells, indolent course, CD5+ B-cell immunophenotype — all point to CLL. The mechanism is BCL2-mediated apoptosis inhibition, not blast accumulation. BCR-ABL1 drives CML (myeloid), JAK2 drives PV (erythroid).

Key take-home messages

What to remember

1.Leukemia is classified by two axes: lineage (myeloid vs lymphoid) AND tempo (acute vs chronic). These two axes determine the dominant cell type, clinical behaviour, and treatment approach.
2.Acute leukaemias = differentiation arrest. Blasts accumulate and cause marrow failure. The triad of anaemia + neutropenia + thrombocytopenia explains almost every clinical feature. A high WBC never means effective immunity in acute leukaemia.
3.TdT positive = lymphoblast (ALL). Auer rods + MPO positive = myeloblast (AML). PAS dot positive = ALL. These are the three most tested morphological and staining differentiators.
4.Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22)/BCR-ABL1 appears in two diseases: CML (defining feature) and ALL (poor prognosis variant). Context — myeloid vs lymphoid, chronic vs acute — determines its significance.
5.CLL is driven by apoptosis inhibition (BCL2), not uncontrolled proliferation. Smudge cells on blood film + CD5/CD19/CD20/CD23 on immunophenotyping = diagnostic. Most patients are asymptomatic at presentation.
6.DIC is specific to APML (AML-M3) — t(15;17)/PML-RARα. It is not a general AML complication. APML requires urgent recognition because its treatment (ATRA) differs fundamentally from all other AML subtypes.
Exam focus

Common traps & rapid recall

Trap 1
t(9;22) appears in two completely different diseases

Students assign Philadelphia chromosome exclusively to CML. However, t(9;22)/BCR-ABL1 also occurs in adult ALL — where it carries a poor prognosis. Always determine: acute (blasts, marrow failure) or chronic (mature cells, indolent)? Clinical context tells you which disease you're dealing with.

Trap 2
High WBC does not mean effective immunity

In acute leukaemia, the elevated WBC consists almost entirely of non-functional blasts. The patient is severely immunocompromised despite an extremely high count. "WBC 95 × 10⁹/L with recurrent severe infections" is a classic exam vignette testing this concept.

Trap 3
DIC is specific to APML (AML-M3), not all AML

When a vignette describes AML with DIC, the answer is AML-M3 (APML) with t(15;17)/PML-RARα. Abnormal promyelocytes trigger systemic DIC through procoagulant granule release. Do not generalise DIC to all AML subtypes.

Trap 4
CML vs leukaemoid reaction — use the LAP score

Both present with WBC >50,000 × 10⁹/L with immature myeloid forms. Key discriminator: LAP score LOW in CML (neoplastic neutrophils lack enzyme); HIGH in leukaemoid reaction (reactive neutrophils have normal activity). Leukaemoid reaction also has no blasts and resolves with treatment.

Trap 5
FAB vs WHO blast threshold for AML diagnosis

FAB required ≥30% blasts; WHO (2001) reduced this to ≥20%. Modern practice uses WHO. If a vignette gives 22% blasts and asks whether AML diagnostic criteria are met — the answer is yes (WHO).

Rapid recall — tap to reveal

What morphological finding is pathognomonic for AML?›
Auer rods — pink rod-like cytoplasmic inclusions in myeloblasts formed from fused azurophilic granules. Never seen in ALL. Their presence on a blood film confirms AML.
What stains differentiate ALL blasts from AML blasts?›
PAS stain: dot positivity in ALL blasts; negative in AML. TdT: positive in ALL; negative in AML. MPO (myeloperoxidase): positive in AML myeloblasts; negative in ALL lymphoblasts.
What is pathognomonic for CLL on blood film and on lymph node biopsy?›
Blood film: smudge cells (Gumprecht shadows) — fragile CLL lymphocytes rupture during smear preparation. Lymph node biopsy: proliferation centres — foci of mitotically active larger cells within sheets of small lymphocytes. Both are pathognomonic for CLL/SLL.
What are the three phases of CML and their blast thresholds?›
Chronic phase: ≤10% blasts (mild/no symptoms). Accelerated phase: 10–19% blasts (basophilia, symptoms emerging). Blast crisis: ≥20% blasts (resembles acute leukaemia; poor prognosis).
Most common cancer in children? Most common adult leukaemia?›
Most common cancer in children: ALL (peak age 2–10 years; >90% cure rate with treatment). Most common adult leukaemia: CLL (predominantly elderly; indolent; often asymptomatic at diagnosis).
