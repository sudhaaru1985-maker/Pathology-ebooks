Learning Outcomes

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  • LO1
    Define anaemia and classify RBC disorders based on pathophysiological mechanisms (decreased production, increased destruction, blood loss).
  • LO2
    Apply mean corpuscular volume (MCV) to categorize anaemia into microcytic, normocytic, and macrocytic types with associated conditions.
  • LO3
    Interpret common peripheral smear findings and correlate them with underlying pathological mechanisms.
  • LO4
    Differentiate key RBC morphological abnormalities (poikilocytosis, anisocytosis, specific cell shapes) in an examination setting.
  • LO5
    Integrate clinical findings with laboratory data (Hb, MCV, reticulocyte count, LDH, bilirubin) to arrive at a logical provisional diagnosis.

Prior Knowledge Bridge

Activate what you already know from Year 1 Physiology & Biochemistry:

🔴 RBC Function

RBCs transport O₂ to peripheral tissues via haemoglobin. Normal lifespan = 120 days.

🏭 Erythropoiesis

RBC production stimulated by hypoxia, controlled by erythropoietin (synthesised in kidneys).

💀 Haemolysis

Destruction of RBCs releases bilirubin. Excess → jaundice. Normal pathway: reticuloendothelial system.

🔬 Maturation Stages

Pronormoblast → Basophilic → Polychromatophilic → Orthochromic → Reticulocyte → Mature RBC.

📊 Key Lab Values

Normal Hb: Men 13.2–16.7 g/dL; Women 11.9–15.0 g/dL. MCV normal range: 81–97 fL.

🩸 Reticulocytes

Young RBCs with residual RNA. Normal count = 1%. Rises when RBC production is stimulated.

Key Conditions & Concepts

Erythropoiesis is the process of RBC production, occurring in the bone marrow. It is stimulated by tissue hypoxia and regulated by erythropoietin (EPO) synthesised in the kidney.

Maturation Sequence

  1. Pronormoblast — Largest cell; large nucleus, basophilic cytoplasm
  2. Basophilic Normoblast — Nucleus begins condensing; cytoplasm still blue
  3. Polychromatophilic Normoblast — Haemoglobin accumulates; mixed cytoplasm staining
  4. Orthochromic Normoblast — Nucleus nearly gone; pink cytoplasm
  5. Reticulocyte (Polychromatophilic RBC) — Nucleus extruded; RNA reticulum remains; lives ~4.8 hrs in circulation
  6. Mature RBC — Biconcave disc; no nucleus; full haemoglobin; lifespan 120 days

Key Changes During Maturation

↓ Changes
Cell size decreases; nuclear size decreases & disappears; chromatin condenses
↑ Changes
Cytoplasm:nucleus ratio increases; cytoplasm staining shifts blue → pinkish-red
📌
Reticulocyte count: Normal = 0.5–1.5%. An elevated count indicates the marrow is actively producing RBCs in response to anaemia or haemolysis.

📘
Definition: A reduction below normal limits of the total circulating red cell mass or haemoglobin concentration (or both), resulting in decreased oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.

Pathophysiological Cascade

Anaemia → Tissue Hypoxia → Two key responses:

↑ EPO Response
Kidney senses hypoxia → ↑ EPO → Bone marrow hyperplasia → ↑ RBC production
Cardiac Compensation
↑ Heart rate + ↑ Cardiac output → Palpitations, tachycardia, cardiac murmur

Symptoms by System

  • Neurological: Headache, vertigo, tinnitus, dizziness
  • Cardiovascular: Palpitations, tachycardia, irregular heartbeat
  • Respiratory: Shortness of breath on exertion
  • Musculoskeletal: Weakness, easy fatigability, malaise
  • Skin/Mucous membranes: Pallor (conjunctivae, nail beds, mucous membranes)
⚠️
Effects of anaemia depend on severity, duration, and rate of development. A slowly developing anaemia (e.g., B12 deficiency) may be well-tolerated at Hb levels that would cause shock if acute. Iron deficiency anaemia is the most common anaemia worldwide.

Size Variants

  • Microcyte (5–6 μm): IDA, thalassaemia
  • Normocyte (7–8 μm): Normal
  • Macrocyte (8–12 μm): B12/folate deficiency, liver disease
  • Megalocyte (>12 μm): Megaloblastic anaemia
  • Schistocyte (2–3 μm): Mechanical fragmentation

Inclusions

  • Howell-Jolly bodies: Post-splenectomy, megaloblastic anaemia (nuclear remnants)
  • Pappenheimer bodies: Iron granules in sideroblastic anaemia
  • Cabot's rings: Remnants of mitotic spindle; megaloblastic anaemia

Intravascular vs Extravascular Haemolysis

Intravascular
RBCs rupture within vessels → free Hb in blood

Features: Haemoglobinaemia, haemoglobinuria, haemosiderinuria, ↓ haptoglobin, jaundice

Causes: Mechanical injury, toxins, complement, parasites
Extravascular
Macrophages in spleen/liver/marrow remove damaged RBCs

Features: Anaemia, splenomegaly, jaundice

Examples: Hereditary spherocytosis, sickle cell, autoimmune haemolytic anaemia
🔑
The key lab marker distinguishing the two: intravascular haemolysis → haemoglobinaemia + ↓↓ serum haptoglobin; extravascular haemolysis → ↑ unconjugated bilirubin + splenomegaly.

Evidence for ↑ Red Cell Production

In the Blood
↑ Reticulocyte count
Circulating nucleated RBCs (NRBCs)
In the Bone Marrow
Erythroid hyperplasia
↓ M:E ratio (normally 3:1 → may become 1:10)
In the Bone (Chronic Haemolysis)
Marrow space expansion → deforming changes in skull and long bones → frontal bossing (seen in thalassaemia major)

Evidence for ↑ Red Cell Destruction

  • ↑ Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) — released from lysed RBCs
  • ↑ Unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin — from haem catabolism
  • ↑ Urobilinogen in urine and stools
  • Morphological evidence on peripheral smear (spherocytes, schistocytes, etc.)
  • Reduced red cell lifespan (measured by radio-labelling)
  • ↓ Serum haptoglobin (in intravascular haemolysis)
📌
Exam tip: LDH is released from RBCs and is non-specific, but combined with ↑ bilirubin + ↑ reticulocytes + peripheral smear morphology, it points strongly towards haemolytic anaemia.

Case 1 — Aishah revisited

19-year-old female | Fatigue, pallor, koilonychia | Hb 7.8 g/dL, MCV 62 fL

Aishah, 19F, presents with 3 months of fatigue, pallor, koilonychia, and angular stomatitis. She has heavy menstrual periods. Investigations: Hb 7.8 g/dL | MCV 62 fL | MCH 18 pg | Reticulocyte count 0.8%.
Q1. What is the most likely classification of this anaemia based on the MCV?
A
Normocytic normochromic anaemia
B
Microcytic hypochromic anaemia
C
Macrocytic normochromic anaemia
D
Haemolytic normocytic anaemia
✅ Correct!
MCV of 62 fL is well below the normal range (81–97 fL), classifying this as microcytic. The low MCH (18 pg, normal 28–34 pg) confirms hypochromia. Together with heavy menstrual periods, koilonychia, and angular stomatitis, this picture is consistent with iron deficiency anaemia — the most common cause of microcytic hypochromic anaemia globally.
❌ Not quite.
Review the MCV value: 62 fL is significantly below the normal range (81–97 fL), placing this firmly in the microcytic category. The low MCH (18 pg) further confirms hypochromia. Normocytic anaemia requires MCV 80–100 fL; macrocytic requires MCV >100 fL. Always use MCV as your first step in classifying anaemia.

Case 2 — Interpreting the Reticulocyte Count

General concept | Normocytic anaemia workup | Bone marrow response

A 35-year-old woman is found to have anaemia (Hb 8.5 g/dL) on routine blood tests. Her MCV is 88 fL (normocytic). Her reticulocyte count comes back at 8%. She has no history of nutritional deficiency or chronic illness. On examination, she is mildly icteric.
Q2. In normocytic anaemia, a high reticulocyte count most likely indicates:
A
Bone marrow failure — the marrow cannot produce enough RBCs
B
Vitamin B12 deficiency causing ineffective erythropoiesis
C
Hyperproliferative response — the marrow is compensating for increased RBC destruction or blood loss
D
Renal failure causing reduced erythropoietin secretion
✅ Correct!
A high reticulocyte count in the context of normocytic anaemia = hyperproliferative response. This means the bone marrow is working hard to compensate, pointing towards haemolytic anaemia or acute blood loss as the underlying cause. In this case, the mild icterus further supports haemolysis (elevated unconjugated bilirubin). In contrast, a low reticulocyte count in normocytic anaemia = hypoproliferative response → think marrow failure, renal failure, or anaemia of chronic disease.
❌ Incorrect.
Bone marrow failure, renal failure, and B12 deficiency all result in a low reticulocyte count — the marrow cannot keep up. A high reticulocyte count means the marrow is actively and successfully ramping up production. In normocytic anaemia, this pattern = hyperproliferative, which should direct you towards haemolysis or acute blood loss as the likely cause.

Case 3 — Sites of Haemolysis

General concept | Intravascular vs extravascular | Distinguishing lab findings

A patient with a known mechanical heart valve develops sudden onset pallor, jaundice, and passes dark brown urine. Blood tests show: Hb 7.2 g/dL | MCV 85 fL | Reticulocyte count 10% | LDH markedly elevated | Unconjugated bilirubin ↑ | Serum haptoglobin undetectable.
Q3. Which of the following findings most specifically indicates intravascular haemolysis rather than extravascular haemolysis?
A
Elevated unconjugated bilirubin
B
Elevated reticulocyte count
C
Haemoglobinuria (dark urine) and undetectable serum haptoglobin
D
Splenomegaly
✅ Correct!
Haemoglobinuria (free Hb in urine → dark/cola-coloured urine) and undetectable haptoglobin are the hallmarks of intravascular haemolysis. When RBCs rupture inside blood vessels, free haemoglobin floods the plasma, saturating haptoglobin (which binds and clears free Hb). Once haptoglobin is exhausted, free Hb spills into urine. In contrast, elevated unconjugated bilirubin, elevated reticulocytes, and splenomegaly occur in both types of haemolysis. Splenomegaly is more a feature of extravascular haemolysis (macrophage-driven).
❌ Not quite.
Elevated unconjugated bilirubin, elevated reticulocyte count, and elevated LDH all occur in both intravascular and extravascular haemolysis — they are non-specific for the site. Splenomegaly is more a feature of extravascular haemolysis. The findings that are specific to intravascular haemolysis are: haemoglobinaemia, haemoglobinuria (dark urine), haemosiderinuria, and markedly reduced/absent serum haptoglobin.

Case 4 — Evidence of Increased RBC Production

General concept | Erythroid hyperplasia | Bone marrow & bone changes

An 8-year-old boy is referred for facial deformity and anaemia. His parents describe recurrent episodes of pallor and jaundice since infancy. On examination, he has a prominent forehead and cheekbone prominence. Blood tests show Hb 7.0 g/dL. Bone marrow biopsy reveals marked erythroid hyperplasia with a myeloid:erythroid (M:E) ratio of 1:8 (normal 3:1).
Q4. The low M:E ratio of 1:8 and the characteristic facial deformity both result from:
A
Reduced erythropoietin secretion by the kidneys
B
Infiltration of the bone marrow by leukaemic cells
C
Chronic compensatory expansion of the erythroid lineage driving marrow space expansion into bone
D
Nutritional deficiency causing ineffective erythropoiesis
✅ Correct!
Chronic haemolytic anaemia → persistent tissue hypoxia → sustained ↑ EPO → erythroid hyperplasia (↑ red cell precursors in marrow → low M:E ratio). When this is severe and long-standing, the expanding erythroid marrow extends into the cortical bones of the skull and face → frontal bossing and facial deformity ("hair-on-end" pattern on skull X-ray). This is a classic finding in thalassaemia major and severe hereditary haemolytic anaemias. It is evidence of the body's attempt to compensate for chronic excessive RBC destruction.
❌ Incorrect.
Reduced EPO (renal failure) and nutritional deficiency both result in a hypoplastic marrow with few red cell precursors — not hyperplasia. Leukaemia would show infiltration by blast cells. The combination of a low M:E ratio (erythroid-dominant marrow) and frontal bossing is explained by chronic erythroid hyperplasia — the marrow's sustained compensatory response to unrelenting haemolysis, expanding to fill and then remodel skeletal bone.

Key Take-Home Messages

1

Anaemia = Sign, Not Diagnosis

Anaemia is always an expression of underlying disease. Always find and treat the cause, not just the low Hb.

2

MCV is Your First Step

Use MCV to classify into microcytic (<80), normocytic (80–100), or macrocytic (>100 fL) — this directs the next investigation.

3

Reticulocyte Count is Key

In normocytic anaemia: High retic = hyperproliferative (haemolysis/blood loss). Low retic = hypoproliferative (marrow failure).

4

Read the Peripheral Smear

Morphology directs diagnosis: bite cells → G6PD; schistocytes → MAHA; spherocytes → HS or AIHA; hyperseg neutrophils → megaloblastic.

5

Haemolysis Has Two Locations

Extravascular = macrophage-mediated (spleen/liver). Intravascular = direct lysis in vessels → haemoglobinaemia, haemoglobinuria, ↓ haptoglobin.

6

IDA is Most Common

Iron deficiency anaemia is the world's most common anaemia. Young women with heavy periods are the highest-risk group.

Exam Focus — Common Traps & Rapid Recall

⚠️

Trap 1: Thalassaemia vs Iron Deficiency — Both Microcytic!

Both have low MCV. Key differences: In IDA → ↓ ferritin, ↑ TIBC, absent iron stores. In thalassaemia → normal/↑ ferritin, ↑ iron stores, HbA₂ elevated (β-thal), RBC count often paradoxically normal or high despite microcytosis (Mentzer index: MCV/RBC <13 suggests thalassaemia; >13 suggests IDA).

⚠️

Trap 2: B12 Deficiency Neurological Symptoms vs Folate Deficiency

Both cause macrocytic megaloblastic anaemia. Only B12 deficiency causes neurological complications (subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord — dorsal columns + corticospinal tracts). Folate deficiency does NOT cause neuropathy. Treating B12 deficiency with folate alone can mask the haematological picture while neurological damage progresses.

⚠️

Trap 3: Reticulocyte Count Interpretation

A normal reticulocyte count (1%) in a severely anaemic patient is actually inappropriately low — the marrow should be responding. Always interpret reticulocyte count in context of the degree of anaemia. A corrected reticulocyte count or reticulocyte production index (RPI) is more accurate.

⚠️

Trap 4: Unconjugated vs Conjugated Bilirubin in Haemolysis

Haemolysis releases haem → converted to unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin — this is what rises in haemolytic anaemia. Conjugated bilirubin elevation occurs in hepatocellular or obstructive (cholestatic) disease. Don't confuse them — the question will test this distinction!

⚠️

Trap 5: Frontal Bossing — Which Anaemia?

Frontal bossing occurs due to erythroid marrow expansion into the skull bones. It is seen in severe chronic haemolytic anaemias, most classically thalassaemia major (β-thalassaemia homozygous). Not typically seen in IDA or aplastic anaemia, as the marrow is not hyperactive.
