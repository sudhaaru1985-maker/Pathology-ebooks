  • Glycogen synthesis (liver)

  • Promotes protein synthesis
  • Released after meals

🧪 Glucose Homeostasis

  • Renal threshold ~180 mg/dL
  • GLUT transporters
  • Insulin receptor signalling
  • HbA1c reflects 2–3 months

  • Autoantibodies (anti-islet)
  • HLA system (MHC class II)
  • Inflammation → tissue damage
  • Molecular mimicry

Section 05

Individual Conditions — Detailed Accordions

Click each accordion to explore the condition in detail. Work through them in order — each builds on the previous.

Definition

Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic β-cells leading to absolute insulin deficiency. Formerly called Juvenile-onset or Insulin-Dependent DM.

Epidemiology

Onset typically <20 years. Normal or underweight at presentation. Accounts for ~10% of all DM cases.

Genetic Susceptibility

Strongly linked to HLA-DR3 and HLA-DR4 (MHC Class II genes on chromosome 6). These HLA alleles present β-cell antigens to autoreactive T-cells, initiating the immune cascade.

Pathogenesis — Step-by-Step
1
Genetic susceptibility HLA-DR3/DR4 alleles confer susceptibility to immune dysregulation targeting β-cells.
2
Environmental trigger Viral infections (Coxsackie B, mumps, rubella) trigger immune activation — possibly via molecular mimicry (viral antigens resemble β-cell antigens).
3
Insulitis CD4+ and CD8+ T-lymphocytes infiltrate the islets (insulitis). Autoantibodies to β-cells and insulin appear in blood — detectable years before symptoms.
4
β-cell destruction T-cell mediated cytotoxicity destroys β-cells. >80–90% of β-cell mass must be lost before overt hyperglycaemia occurs.
5
Absolute insulin deficiency Results in uncontrolled hyperglycaemia, lipolysis, and if untreated → diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
Morphology of Islets in Type 1 DM
EARLY (Insulitis phase) Marked lymphocytic infiltration of islets (insulitis). Islets appear active, inflamed.
LATE (Established DM) Marked islet atrophy and fibrosis. Near-total depletion of β-cells. α-cells preserved.
Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) — Type 1 Emergency

Without insulin, lipolysis is uncontrolled → free fatty acids → ketone bodies (acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate, acetone). Results in:

Glucose
>250 mg/dL (often 500–800)
Acidosis
pH <7.30, HCO₃⁻ <15
Kussmaul
Deep rapid breathing to blow off CO₂
Fruity breath
Acetone smell — key dental sign
Nausea / vomiting
Abdominal pain
Coma
Mental status changes → coma

Precipitants: Infection, missed insulin dose, physiological stress (trauma, surgery).

🎬 Video Resource — Understanding Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)
Definition

Combination of peripheral insulin resistance and relative insulin deficiency due to progressive β-cell exhaustion. Not autoimmune.

Epidemiology

Onset typically >30 years. Usually obese (BMI >25). No HLA linkage. Strongly influenced by lifestyle factors.

Risk Factors for Insulin Resistance
  • 🔸 Overweight/obesity (BMI >25)
  • 🔸 Family history of T2DM
  • 🔸 Hypertension
  • 🔸 Metabolic syndrome
  • 🔸 Low HDL / high triglycerides
  • 🔸 Polycystic ovary disease (PCOD)
  • 🔸 Previous gestational DM
  • 🔸 Physical inactivity
Pathogenesis — Step-by-Step
1
Genetic predisposition + Obesity/Lifestyle Adipose tissue releases FFA, adipokines, and pro-inflammatory cytokines that impair insulin receptor signalling.
2
Insulin resistance Liver, muscle, and adipose cells fail to respond normally to insulin. Normal insulin levels cannot maintain normoglycaemia.
3
Compensatory β-cell hyperplasia β-cells increase insulin production to overcome resistance → normoglycaemia maintained temporarily.
4
Early β-cell failure β-cells can no longer compensate → impaired glucose tolerance (pre-diabetes). First-phase insulin secretion lost.
5
Late β-cell exhaustion Chronic overwork leads to β-cell apoptosis and amyloid deposition in islets → overt T2DM. In very late stages, insulin injections may be needed (IDDM-like).
Morphology of Islets in Type 2 DM
NO INSULITIS No lymphocytic infiltration (not autoimmune).
FOCAL ATROPHY + AMYLOID Islet amyloid polypeptide (amylin) deposits. Mild β-cell depletion only.
Hyperglycaemic Hyperosmolar State (HHS) — Type 2 Emergency

Unlike DKA, there is enough insulin to prevent ketogenesis but not enough to utilise all glucose. Serum glucose 400–1800 mg/dL. No ketoacidosis. Precipitated by stress, infection, or dehydration. High mortality if untreated.

🎬 Video Resource — Hyperglycaemic Hyperosmolar State (HHS)

Persistent hyperglycaemia damages tissues through three main biochemical pathways. Understanding these explains why specific organs are targeted.

Mechanism 1 — Non-Enzymatic Glycation (AGEs)

Glucose-derived precursors (glyoxal, methylglyoxal, 3-deoxyglucosone) react non-enzymatically with amino groups of proteins → Advanced Glycation End-products (AGEs). AGEs bind to RAGE receptors on cells and cause:

  • 🔴 Release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, TNF-α)
  • 🔴 Generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in endothelium
  • 🔴 Increased procoagulant activity → thrombosis
  • 🔴 Enhanced vascular smooth muscle cell proliferation
  • 🔴 Cross-linking of extracellular matrix proteins → vessel stiffening

Dental relevance: Collagen cross-linking in periodontal ligament impairs regeneration and wound healing post-extraction.

Mechanism 2 — Polyol Pathway (Sorbitol Accumulation)

In insulin-independent tissues (nerves, retina, kidneys, blood vessels), excess glucose is converted to sorbitol by aldose reductase (requires NADPH). Sorbitol cannot cross cell membranes → intracellular accumulation:

  • 🔵 Osmotic stress → cell swelling → damage (lens → cataracts; nerves → neuropathy)
  • 🔵 NADPH depletion → reduced glutathione → oxidative damage
  • 🔵 Decreased myoinositol → altered signal transduction in neurons

Key mnemonic: Sorbitol = Stays Inside → Swells the cell. Targets: Schwartzian nerves, Retina, Kidney, Lens (Schwann cells, Retina, Kidney, Lens).

Mechanism 3 — Protein Kinase C (PKC) Activation

Hyperglycaemia increases intracellular diacylglycerol (DAG) → activates PKC. PKC activation leads to:

  • 🟢 ↑ VEGF production → neovascularisation (retinopathy)
  • 🟢 ↑ Endothelin-1 → vasoconstriction
  • 🟢 ↓ Nitric oxide → endothelial dysfunction
  • 🟢 ↑ TGF-β → basement membrane thickening (nephropathy)
  • 🟢 ↑ Pro-inflammatory cytokines → atherosclerosis acceleration

Microvascular disease is the hallmark of DM and results from the biochemical mechanisms above acting on small vessels. Key features: basement membrane thickening, pericyte loss, increased vascular permeability.

1. Diabetic Retinopathy — Leading cause of blindness in working-age adults
Non-proliferative (background) Microaneurysms → dot haemorrhages → hard exudates → macular oedema
Proliferative VEGF ↑ → new vessel formation → vitreous haemorrhage → retinal detachment → blindness

Other ocular complications: cataracts (sorbitol in lens), glaucoma (neovascularisation of iris).

2. Diabetic Nephropathy — Leading cause of end-stage renal disease
  • 🔵 Earliest sign: microalbuminuria (30–300 mg/day)
  • 🔵 Kimmelstiel-Wilson lesion: nodular glomerulosclerosis — pathognomonic for diabetic nephropathy
  • 🔵 Diffuse glomerulosclerosis (more common but less specific)
  • 🔵 Hyaline arteriosclerosis of afferent AND efferent arterioles (efferent involvement is unique to DM)
  • 🔵 Progression: microalbuminuria → gross albuminuria → nephrotic syndrome → renal failure
3. Diabetic Neuropathy — Leading cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputation
Peripheral neuropathy Glove-and-stocking sensory loss. Loss of pain/temperature → unnoticed foot injuries → gangrene.
Autonomic neuropathy Gastroparesis, orthostatic hypotension, impotence, silent MI (no chest pain due to cardiac denervation).
Key Point — Dyslipidaemia as the Driver

Diabetic dyslipidaemia: ↓ HDL + ↑ Triglycerides (most common pattern). Low HDL is the most constant predictor of cardiovascular disease in DM. This, combined with AGE-mediated endothelial damage and PKC-driven inflammation, accelerates atherosclerosis.

Major Macrovascular Manifestations
Myocardial Infarction
Most common cause of death in diabetics. May be silent due to autonomic neuropathy.
Stroke
2–4× increased risk of CVA compared to non-diabetics.
Peripheral Vascular Disease
Gangrene of lower extremities 100× more common in diabetics. Dry gangrene → amputation.
Hyaline Arteriosclerosis
More severe in diabetics. Affects afferent AND efferent renal arterioles.
Dental Relevance
Poor perfusion → impaired healing. Increased risk of post-extraction alveolitis. Accelerated periodontitis.
HHS Risk
Type 2 patients under surgical/dental stress may precipitate HHS — glucose monitoring essential.
Why Diabetics are Infection-Prone

Hyperglycaemia impairs neutrophil function (reduced chemotaxis, phagocytosis, oxidative burst), T-cell responses, and complement activity. The high glucose environment also promotes microbial growth.

HbA1c reflects average blood glucose over the preceding 2–3 months (lifespan of RBC). Glucose non-enzymatically glycates haemoglobin — higher average glucose = higher HbA1c.

🦷 Dental Management of Diabetic Patients
  • ⚠️ Check BGL before invasive treatment (safe range: 80–180 mg/dL)
  • ⚠️ Morning appointments preferred (cortisol spike less likely, patient has eaten)
  • ⚠️ Do not proceed if fasting BGL >400 or patient in DKA/HHS
  • ⚠️ Prophylactic antibiotics for extensive surgical procedures
  • ⚠️ Stress reduction protocol — stress raises blood glucose
  • ⚠️ Warn patient about hypoglycaemia if on insulin (keep glucose tablets)

Application Cases with Stepwise MCQs

Work through each clinical case step by step. Click an option to reveal feedback before proceeding to the next question.

📋 Case 1 — The Thirsty Teenager

Amir, 14 years old, presents to the dental clinic for his first visit in 2 years. He has generalised gingival bleeding, and his mother reports he has been drinking water constantly and urinating frequently. He lost 5 kg in 6 weeks. His random blood glucose is 480 mg/dL. He has no prior diagnosis of diabetes.

Step 1 of 4
1. Based on the clinical presentation, which type of diabetes mellitus is most likely in this patient?
✓ Correct! Option B Young age (<20 years), acute onset with "three polys," significant weight loss despite eating, and markedly elevated glucose strongly suggest Type 1 DM. The patient is thin (not obese), making T2DM unlikely. MODY presents more insidiously. No mention of steroid use.
✗ Incorrect. Consider the age (14 years), acute onset, and weight loss with polyphagia. These features — especially in a young, non-obese patient — point to Type 1 DM, where absolute insulin deficiency leads to catabolism. The correct answer is B.
Step 2 of 4
2. What is the PRIMARY immune mechanism responsible for β-cell destruction in this patient's condition?
✓ Correct! Option C Type 1 DM is a classic Type IV (cell-mediated) hypersensitivity. CD4+ and CD8+ T-lymphocytes infiltrate islets (insulitis) and destroy β-cells via direct cytotoxicity and cytokine release. Autoantibodies (anti-GAD, anti-IA2) are present but serve as markers, not primary mediators of destruction.
✗ Incorrect. Type 1 DM is a Type IV (delayed-type/cell-mediated) hypersensitivity reaction. T-lymphocytes — both CD4+ helper and CD8+ cytotoxic — attack β-cells directly. This is why it is called "autoimmune insulitis." The correct answer is C.
Step 3 of 4
3. Amir's breath has a sweet, fruity odour. What metabolic state does this indicate, and what is the underlying mechanism?
✓ Correct! Option B Fruity/acetone breath is the hallmark of DKA. Absolute insulin deficiency → unchecked lipolysis → free fatty acids → β-oxidation → excess acetyl-CoA → ketone bodies (acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate, acetone). Acetone is volatile and exhaled, producing the characteristic fruity odour. This is a dental emergency — refer immediately.
✗ Incorrect. Fruity/acetone breath = ketones = DKA (Option B). In HHS (Type 2), there is enough residual insulin to suppress lipolysis, so ketones do NOT form and breath is not fruity. Uremia produces ammonia/ammoniacal breath, not fruity. This is critical for dental emergencies.
Step 4 of 4
4. Which gene locus is most strongly associated with susceptibility to Type 1 DM, and what is its role?
✓ Correct! Option C HLA-DR3 and HLA-DR4 alleles on chromosome 6p21 are the strongest genetic risk factors for T1DM. These MHC Class II molecules present β-cell autoantigens (e.g., GAD65, insulin) to CD4+ T-cells, initiating the autoimmune cascade. TCF7L2 and PPARG are linked to T2DM; KCNJ11 mutations cause MODY/neonatal DM.
✗ Incorrect. The answer is C — HLA-DR3/DR4. This is the IDDM1 locus. MHC Class II molecules encoded by these alleles present islet peptides to T-cells, initiating autoimmune β-cell attack. TCF7L2 and PPARG are T2DM genes; KCNJ11 relates to MODY/neonatal DM.
📋 Case 2 — The Middle-Aged Executive

Mr. Tan, 52 years old, overweight (BMI 29), presents for a full-mouth rehabilitation. He mentions he was told his blood sugar is "a bit high" but has not started any medication. His fasting blood glucose today is 142 mg/dL. He has deep periodontal pockets (6–8 mm), and his sockets from previous extractions have been "slow to heal."

Step 1 of 3
5. Mr. Tan's fasting glucose of 142 mg/dL indicates which metabolic state?
✓ Correct! Option C A fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL, confirmed on two separate occasions, meets the WHO/ADA diagnostic criteria for DM. Mr. Tan's value of 142 mg/dL exceeds this threshold. Pre-diabetes (IGT) is 100–125 mg/dL. A hyperglycaemic crisis requires glucose typically >250+ with symptoms of DKA or HHS.
✗ Incorrect. 142 mg/dL fasting exceeds the ≥126 mg/dL DM diagnostic threshold (Option C). Pre-diabetes is 100–125 mg/dL. Normal is <100 mg/dL. This patient has undiagnosed Type 2 DM — he should be referred to his physician before extensive dental rehabilitation.
Step 2 of 3
6. Which biochemical mechanism BEST explains why Mr. Tan has impaired post-extraction healing and severe periodontitis?
✓ Correct! Option B AGEs are central to wound healing failure in DM. They cross-link collagen in periodontal ligament and alveolar bone matrix (preventing remodelling), impair neutrophil chemotaxis and phagocytosis (allowing bacterial overgrowth), reduce endothelial NO (impairing angiogenesis), and activate RAGE → pro-inflammatory cytokines → accelerated periodontal tissue breakdown.
✗ Incorrect. The key mechanism is B — AGE-mediated damage. AGEs cross-link collagen (poor matrix remodelling), impair neutrophils (infection susceptibility), and reduce angiogenesis (poor healing). Sorbitol affects nerves/eyes/kidneys, not primarily bone. Clotting factors are not reduced in DM.
Step 3 of 3
7. Mr. Tan asks what test the physician will use to assess how well his blood sugar has been controlled over the last 3 months. What is the CORRECT answer?
✓ Correct! Option C HbA1c (glycosylated haemoglobin) is the gold standard for monitoring long-term glycaemic control. Glucose non-enzymatically binds to haemoglobin — higher average glucose = higher HbA1c. Since RBCs live ~120 days, HbA1c reflects average BGL over 2–3 months. Target: <7% for good control. As a dentist, knowing the HbA1c helps predict healing outcomes.
✗ Incorrect. HbA1c (Option C) is the answer. It reflects average glycaemia over 2–3 months, unlike fasting glucose (single snapshot). C-peptide measures insulin secretion capacity. Urine microalbumin screens for nephropathy, not glycaemic control. HbA1c <7% = good control; >8% = expect significantly impaired healing.

Section 08

Key Take-Home Messages

These are the core concepts you must own before your assessment. Each links mechanism to clinical consequence.

01
DM = Hyperglycaemia from absolute OR relative insulin deficiency "Starvation amidst plenty" — cells starve despite high serum glucose because insulin is absent/ineffective for glucose uptake.
02
Type 1 = Autoimmune; Type 2 = Resistance then exhaustion T-cell mediated β-cell destruction (T1) vs. insulin resistance progressing to β-cell failure (T2). Different pathogenesis → different complications timeline.
03
Three mechanisms drive all complications: AGEs, Polyol pathway, PKC AGEs = cross-linking and inflammation. Sorbitol = osmotic damage to nerves, eye, kidney. PKC = neovascularisation and endothelial dysfunction.
04
Microvascular targets: retina, kidney, nerve = the "3 Rs": Retinopathy, Renal disease (nephropathy), neuropathy Each is a leading cause of major disability: blindness, ESRD, and amputation respectively.
05
Macrovascular disease kills most diabetics — MI is #1 cause of death Low HDL + high triglycerides + endothelial AGE damage → accelerated atherosclerosis. Gangrene 100× more common.
06
HbA1c <7% = good control; measures average glucose over 2–3 months Each 1% increase in HbA1c corresponds to ~30 mg/dL average glucose increase. Essential for predicting dental healing outcomes.
07
DKA (Type 1) vs HHS (Type 2) — different emergencies, same urgency DKA: fruity breath + acidosis + ketones. HHS: extreme hyperglycaemia + no ketones + hyperosmolarity. Both require urgent referral from dental chair.
08
Oral consequences are significant: periodontitis ↔ DM is bidirectional DM worsens periodontal disease; treating periodontitis can improve HbA1c. Every dental patient's glycaemic status matters clinically.
Section 09

Exam Focus — Common Traps & Rapid Recall

These are the most commonly missed concepts at Year 2 level. Know each trap and the correct answer.

⚠️
Common Exam Trap
Trap 1: "Type 2 DM can never need insulin"
WRONG. In late-stage Type 2 DM, β-cell exhaustion can lead to IDDM-like absolute deficiency requiring insulin injections. The distinction is that T2DM does NOT start insulin-dependent — it becomes so only in advanced stages.
⚠️
Common Exam Trap
Trap 2: Confusing DKA with HHS
DKA = Type 1, absolute insulin absence → ketones → acidosis → fruity breath. HHS = Type 2, residual insulin prevents ketones → no fruity breath → extreme hyperglycaemia (400–1800 mg/dL). Fruity breath = DKA = Type 1 = refer immediately.
⚠️
Common Exam Trap
Trap 3: Assuming autoantibodies CAUSE β-cell destruction in T1DM
Anti-islet antibodies (anti-GAD, anti-IA2, ICA) are markers, not the primary mediators. β-cell destruction is T-lymphocyte mediated (Type IV hypersensitivity). Antibodies appear in blood years before clinical DM but serve as predictive biomarkers, not causative agents.
⚠️
Common Exam Trap
Trap 4: Thinking sorbitol affects all organs equally
The polyol pathway only damages tissues where glucose entry does NOT require insulin: nerves, retina, lens, kidney, blood vessel walls. Muscle and liver (which need insulin for GLUT4) are NOT targets of sorbitol accumulation because they don't have excess intracellular glucose when insulin is absent.
⚠️
Common Exam Trap
Trap 5: Kimmelstiel-Wilson lesions are non-specific
Nodular glomerulosclerosis (Kimmelstiel-Wilson lesion) is PATHOGNOMONIC for diabetic nephropathy — seen in no other disease. Diffuse glomerulosclerosis is more common but less specific. If an exam shows a nodule in the glomerulus → diabetic nephropathy.
⚠️
Common Exam Trap
Trap 6: Silent MI is a normal MI that the patient ignores
Silent MI in diabetics occurs because autonomic neuropathy damages cardiac afferent pain fibres. The patient genuinely does NOT feel chest pain. This is pathological denervation — clinically crucial as the first sign may be sudden cardiac death.
