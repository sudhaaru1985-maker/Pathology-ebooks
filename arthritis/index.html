Section 02

Prior Knowledge Bridge

Before diving in, recall these foundational concepts from Year 1 and earlier Year 2 modules.

🦴 Joint Anatomy

Synovial joints consist of articular cartilage (type II collagen + proteoglycans), synovial membrane (secretes synovial fluid), joint capsule, and subchondral bone. Cartilage is avascular and relies on diffusion for nutrition.

🔥 Inflammation Basics

Acute inflammation involves vascular changes, neutrophil recruitment, and chemical mediators (PGE₂, LTB₄). Chronic inflammation involves lymphocytes, plasma cells, and macrophages. Granulomatous inflammation features palisaded macrophages (histiocytes) around a necrotic core.

🧬 Autoimmunity

Loss of immune tolerance leads to autoreactive T and B cells attacking self-antigens. HLA molecules (MHC class II) present antigens to CD4+ T helper cells (Th1, Th17). Cytokines like TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IL-17, and IFN-γ drive tissue destruction.

🧫 Purine Metabolism

Purines (adenine, guanine) are degraded to uric acid via xanthine oxidase. Uric acid is the final breakdown product in humans (no uricase). Normal serum urate ≤6.8 mg/dL. Hyperuricemia → monosodium urate (MSU) crystal deposition in cool peripheral joints.

🩺 Matrix Metalloproteinases

MMPs are zinc-dependent enzymes that degrade extracellular matrix components (collagen, proteoglycans). They are upregulated by pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α) and play a central role in both OA and RA cartilage destruction.

🦠 HLA Associations

HLA-DR4/DR1 → Rheumatoid arthritis (class II, activates CD4+ T cells). HLA-B27 → Seronegative spondyloarthropathies (class I, activates CD8+ T cells). These genetic associations explain familial clustering and variable disease expression.

Section 03

Clinical Trigger

📋 Case Scenario A

A 49-year-old man presents with a 10-year history of episodic joint pain involving his hands and feet in a remitting and relapsing pattern. On examination, his hands show swan-neck deformities and ulnar deviation of the fingers. Laboratory investigations reveal elevated ESR, positive rheumatoid factor, and anti-CCP antibodies. His symptoms significantly improve following methotrexate therapy.

Think about: What type of arthritis presents symmetrically with small joint involvement, systemic features, and responds to DMARDs? What autoantibodies would you request?
📋 Case Scenario B

An otherwise healthy 44-year-old man has increasing back pain and right hip pain for a decade. Pain is worse at end of day and after activity. Examination shows bony enlargement of DIP joints. X-ray spine shows prominent osteophytes on vertebral bodies; X-ray pelvis shows sclerosis and joint space narrowing at the right acetabulum. Inflammatory markers are normal.

Think about: Which arthritis is degenerative, worsens with use, affects weight-bearing joints, lacks systemic features, and shows osteophytes on imaging?

Section 05

Disease / Condition Accordions

🦴
Osteoarthritis (OA)
Degenerative Joint Disease
▼
Definition

A degenerative joint disease characterised by progressive degeneration of articular cartilage, with secondary changes in the subchondral bone and periarticular structures. It is not primarily inflammatory but involves low-grade synovitis.

Affected Joints
  • Hip and knee (weight-bearing)
  • Cervical and lumbar spine
  • DIP joints → Heberden's nodes
  • PIP joints → Bouchard's nodes
  • First carpometacarpal joint
Pathogenesis (Step-by-step)
1
Chondrocyte injury: Obesity, aging, trauma, microfractures, and anatomic abnormalities lead to mechanical stress on chondrocytes, generating reactive oxygen species and impaired repair.
2
Cartilage degradation: Stressed chondrocytes release MMPs (collagenase, stromelysin) and aggrecanases driven by PGE₂, NO, and TNF. IL-8 and BMPs are released. Degradation exceeds repair — type II collagen and proteoglycans are lost.
3
Fragmentation & fibrillation: The cartilage surface cracks and fragments, producing loose bodies (joint mice) that irritate the synovium.
4
Subchondral bone changes: Exposed subchondral bone undergoes eburnation (polished ivory appearance from friction), subchondral cyst formation, and sclerosis. Bone remodels with osteophyte formation at joint margins.
5
Low-grade synovitis: Cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α) from the synovium perpetuate cartilage degradation, though synovitis is far less severe than in RA.
Morphology (Gross)
  • Fibrillation of articular cartilage (surface cracks)
  • Eburnated articular surface (ivory-like subchondral bone)
  • Subchondral cysts (fluid-filled spaces)
  • Osteophytes (bony spurs at joint margins)
  • Loose bodies ("joint mice")
Morphology (Histology)
  • Loss of basophilic staining (proteoglycan depletion)
  • Vertical clefts in cartilage (fibrillation)
  • Chondrocyte cloning (clusters)
  • Chondrocyte dropout and apoptosis (late)
  • Subchondral bone changes — microfractures, sclerosis
Clinical Features
  • Deep, aching joint pain — worsens with use, eases with rest
  • Morning stiffness <30 minutes (key: brief)
  • Crepitus (audible/palpable grinding)
  • Bony enlargement — Heberden's (DIP) & Bouchard's (PIP) nodes
  • Small joint effusions
  • Limitation of movement; NO systemic features
Risk Factors
  • Older age (risk ↑ from 40 years)
  • Obesity (excess mechanical load)
  • Joint injury / long-term overuse
  • Family history (genetic predisposition)
  • Malformed joints (e.g., knock-knees, congenital hip dysplasia)
  • Physical inactivity (weak periarticular muscles)
Complications
  • Nerve root compression by osteophytes → radicular pain, neurological deficits
  • Joint deformity and instability
  • Muscle spasm and atrophy
  • Significant disability (hip/knee OA)
🛡️
Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
Chronic Systemic Autoimmune Arthritis
▼
Definition

A chronic, systemic, autoimmune disorder principally affecting joints, producing a non-suppurative proliferative and inflammatory synovitis that progresses to destruction of articular cartilage, fibrous ankylosis, and bony ankylosis. Extraarticular lesions occur in skin, heart, lungs, and blood vessels.

Etiology / Genetics
  • HLA-DR4 / HLA-DR1 (class II MHC) — 50% risk contribution
  • PTPN22 alleles (immune regulation gene)
  • Environmental triggers: smoking, infections
  • Loss of tolerance → citrullination of self-proteins
Pathogenesis (Step-by-step)
1
Loss of tolerance: Genetic predisposition + environmental triggers (infection, smoking) → enzymatic citrullination of self-proteins (e.g., fibrinogen, vimentin) → development of anti-citrullinated protein antibodies (ACPA/anti-CCP) and rheumatoid factor (RF, auto-Ab against Fc of IgG).
2
T cell & B cell activation: Th1 cells release IFN-γ → activates synovial fibroblasts and macrophages. Th17 cells release IL-17 → recruits neutrophils and monocytes. B cells produce RF and ACPA.
3
Synovial inflammation: TNF-α and IL-1 → stimulate synovial fibroblasts and chondrocytes to release collagenase, stromelysin, elastase, PGE₂ → destroy cartilage. IL-6 drives acute phase response (elevated ESR, CRP).
4
Pannus formation: Hyperplastic synovial tissue (pannus) composed of inflammatory cells, granulation tissue, and fibroblast-like synoviocytes grows over articular cartilage → direct erosion.
5
Bone destruction: RANKL expressed on T cells and synoviocytes → activates osteoclasts → periarticular bone resorption and erosions.
6
Ankylosis: Persistent destruction → fibrous ankylosis (fibrous tissue bridges joint) → bony ankylosis (bone bridges joint space).
Joint Morphology
  • Synovial hyperplasia with villous projections
  • Dense infiltration: T cells (Th1/Th17), B cells, plasma cells, macrophages, dendritic cells
  • Lymphoid aggregates (germinal centres) in synovium
  • Increased vascularity (angiogenesis)
  • Rice bodies — organized fibrin fragments floating in joint fluid
  • Neutrophils in synovial fluid
  • Pannus → fibrous ankylosis → bony ankylosis
Extraarticular Morphology
  • Rheumatoid nodules (skin): areas of fibrinoid necrosis surrounded by a palisade of macrophages (histiocytes) and lymphocytes. Located at pressure areas: ulnar forearm, elbows, occiput, lumbosacral area
  • Vasculitis: peripheral neuropathy, skin ulcers, gangrene
  • Lungs: pleuritis, interstitial fibrosis
  • Heart: pericarditis
  • Kidneys: AA amyloidosis (from chronic inflammation)
Clinical Features (Joints)
  • Females > Males; any age (including children — Still's disease)
  • Symmetrical polyarthritis — small joints first (MCP, PIP), then larger joints
  • Morning stiffness >1 hour (key distinguishing feature)
  • Swollen, warm, painful joints
  • Swan-neck deformity (PIP hyperextension, DIP flexion)
  • Boutonnière deformity (PIP flexion, DIP hyperextension)
  • Ulnar deviation of MCP joints
  • X-ray: diffuse osteopenia, joint space loss, periarticular erosions, ulnar drift
Extraarticular Manifestations
  • Ocular: keratoconjunctivitis sicca, scleritis, episcleritis
  • Pulmonary: pleural disease, interstitial lung disease
  • Cardiac: pericarditis, coronary artery disease
  • Renal: AA amyloid, membranous GN, tubulo-interstitial nephritis
  • Haematological: anaemia of chronic disease, Felty's syndrome (RA + splenomegaly + neutropaenia)
  • Neurological: entrapment neuropathy (carpal tunnel), cervical myelopathy
Laboratory Investigations
  • Rheumatoid Factor (RF): positive in ~80%
  • Anti-CCP (anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide): positive in ~70%, more specific
  • Elevated ESR, CRP (active inflammation)
  • Normocytic normochromic anaemia
  • X-ray / MRI for erosions and joint damage
💎
Gouty Arthritis
Crystalline Arthritis · MSU Crystal Deposition
▼
Definition

A metabolic disorder characterised by transient attacks of acute arthritis initiated by deposition of monosodium urate (MSU) crystals in and around joints. Requires sustained hyperuricaemia (serum urate >6.8 mg/dL).

Classification
  • Primary Gout (90%): Overproduction (diet, unknown enzyme defects, partial HGPRT deficiency) OR reduced renal excretion with normal production
  • Secondary Gout (10%): Overproduction (leukemias/neoplasms → high nucleic acid turnover; Lesch-Nyhan syndrome — complete HGPRT deficiency) OR reduced excretion (chronic renal disease)
Pathogenesis (Step-by-step)
1
Hyperuricaemia: Excessive uric acid production or decreased renal excretion → serum urate supersaturation.
2
Crystal precipitation: MSU crystals deposit in cooler areas (first MTP joint — podagra, ankle, wrist). Triggers include dehydration, trauma, alcohol, sudden dietary change.
3
Macrophage activation: MSU crystals are phagocytosed by macrophages → activates NLRP3 inflammasome → releases IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6 → releases LTB₄, prostaglandins, free radicals → neutrophil chemotaxis.
4
Neutrophil cycle: Neutrophils phagocytose crystals → lysis of neutrophils → release of lysosomal enzymes and crystals → perpetuates inflammation → tissue injury.
5
Resolution: Anti-inflammatory mechanisms (lipoxin production, TGF-β) → resolution of acute attack, but recurrence leads to chronic disease.
Risk Factors
  • Male sex (4× more common); women post-menopause
  • Age: men 30–50 years
  • High-purine diet: red meat, shellfish, organ meat
  • Alcohol (especially beer — contains purines; reduces urate excretion)
  • Obesity
  • Drugs: thiazide diuretics, aspirin (low dose), ciclosporin
  • HGPRT deficiency, Lesch-Nyhan syndrome
Morphology
  • Acute arthritis: MSU crystals in joint fluid; neutrophilic synovitis; needle-shaped, negatively birefringent crystals (yellow when parallel to slow axis of polarizer)
  • Tophi: Aggregates of MSU crystals surrounded by granulomatous inflammation (palisaded macrophages and foreign body giant cells)
  • Chronic tophaceous arthritis: Chalky deposits in synovium; synovial hyperplasia and fibrosis
  • Gouty nephropathy: Urate crystal deposition in renal interstitium and tubules; can lead to nephrolithiasis
  • Tophi in other sites: Tendons, ligaments, bursae, ear pinnae
Clinical Stages
  • Asymptomatic hyperuricaemia: No joint symptoms yet
  • Acute gouty arthritis: Sudden onset, severe pain, redness, swelling of first MTP (podagra) — often nocturnal; self-limiting in 3–10 days
  • Intercritical gout: Asymptomatic intervals between attacks
  • Chronic tophaceous gout: Persistent joint damage, tophus formation, deformity; develops years after first attack
Pseudogout (CPPD) — Compare
  • Calcium pyrophosphate crystal deposition
  • Rhomboid-shaped crystals (NOT needle-shaped)
  • Positively birefringent on polarising microscopy
  • Chalky white friable deposits in articular cartilage
  • Affects knees most commonly (compare to gout → first MTP)
🔗
Seronegative Spondyloarthropathies
HLA-B27 Associated · RF-Negative
▼
Shared Features (Heterogeneous Group)
  • Absence of Rheumatoid Factor (seronegative)
  • Pathology in ligaments (enthesitis) rather than synovium
  • Association with HLA-B27
  • Sacroiliac joint involvement (sacroiliitis)
  • Ankylosis of joints
  • Pathogenesis: T cell response against unknown antigen (triggered by infection) cross-reacts with joint antigens
Types
  • Ankylosing Spondylitis: Bridging spinal osteophytes, sacroiliitis, enthesitis, uveitis; bamboo spine on X-ray; 90% HLA-B27 positive
  • Reactive Arthritis (Reiter's): Triad — arthritis + non-gonococcal urethritis/cervicitis + conjunctivitis; follows Chlamydia, Shigella, Salmonella, Campylobacter, Yersinia infection
  • Psoriatic Arthropathy: Arthritis with psoriatic skin disease; DIP joint involvement characteristic
  • Enteropathic Arthropathy: Ulcerative colitis, Crohn's disease associated
  • Juvenile Ankylosing Spondylitis
🦠
Infectious (Suppurative) Arthritis
Bacterial & Viral Joint Infections
▼
Routes of Infection
  • Haematogenous (most common)
  • Direct inoculation through skin (trauma, surgery)
  • Spread from soft tissue abscess or osteomyelitis
Clinical Features
  • Sudden onset — acutely painful, warm, swollen joint
  • Restricted range of motion
  • Fever and neutrophilic leucocytosis
  • Can cause rapid permanent joint destruction if untreated
Age-Based Organism Pattern
  • Neonates and infants → Haemophilus influenzae
  • Older children and adults → Staphylococcus aureus (most common overall)
  • Sickle cell disease patients → Salmonella
  • Sexually active young women → Neisseria gonorrhoeae (gonococcal arthritis)
  • Intravenous drug abusers → involves axial joints; often unusual organisms
Section 06

Application Cases with Stepwise MCQs

Case 1 · Osteoarthritis

The Retired Farmer with Knee Pain

A 68-year-old obese male farmer presents with a 5-year history of worsening right knee pain. The pain is dull and aching, worsened by prolonged standing and climbing stairs, and partially relieved by rest. He reports brief morning stiffness lasting about 15 minutes. On examination, there is bony enlargement of the DIP joints of both hands and audible crepitus on knee flexion. No systemic symptoms. X-ray shows joint space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis, and osteophytes. ESR and CRP are normal.
Step 1: Which pathological process best explains the ivory-like appearance of the articular surface seen on gross examination of this patient's knee?
✅ Correct — B. Eburnation is the hallmark gross finding in OA. When articular cartilage is fully lost, the exposed subchondral bone is subjected to repeated friction, becoming polished and ivory-like. This is distinct from pannus (RA), tophaceous deposits (gout), or fibrinoid necrosis (RA nodules).
❌ Incorrect. Remember the gross pathology sequence in OA: cartilage fibrillation → fragmentation → full thickness loss → subchondral bone is exposed → friction produces the polished ivory appearance called eburnation. Pannus is an RA feature; chalky deposits are gout/pseudogout; fibrinoid necrosis is seen in RA nodules.
Step 2: The bony swellings on the DIP joints are called Heberden's nodes. What do they represent histologically?
✅ Correct — C. Heberden's nodes (DIP) and Bouchard's nodes (PIP) are bony enlargements caused by marginal osteophyte formation in OA. They represent reactive bone proliferation at joint margins as the body attempts to stabilise the degenerating joint. They are NOT tophi (gout), rheumatoid nodules (RA), or synovial cysts (ganglion).
❌ Incorrect. Heberden's nodes = osteophytes at DIP joints in OA. Keep this distinct from: tophi (gout — MSU crystals + granulomatous inflammation), rheumatoid nodules (RA — fibrinoid necrosis + palisaded macrophages), and ganglion cysts (synovial fluid-filled cysts near tendon sheaths).
Step 3: Which combination of features most strongly supports a diagnosis of OA over RA in this patient?
✅ Correct — C. OA is characterised by: pain that worsens with use (not rest), morning stiffness <30 minutes (RA is >1 hour), normal inflammatory markers (ESR/CRP), and X-ray findings of osteophytes + subchondral sclerosis + joint space narrowing. Options A, B, and D all describe RA features.
❌ Incorrect. The key OA vs RA differentiators are: (1) Stiffness duration — OA <30 min; RA >1 hour; (2) Inflammatory markers — OA normal; RA elevated; (3) Autoantibodies — OA absent; RA has RF and anti-CCP; (4) X-ray — OA shows osteophytes; RA shows erosions and periarticular osteopenia.
Case 2 · Rheumatoid Arthritis

The Young Woman with Stiff Morning Hands

A 32-year-old female teacher presents with a 6-month history of pain and swelling in both hands and wrists. She reports significant morning stiffness lasting over 2 hours, bilateral involvement, fatigue, and a low-grade fever. On examination, both MCP and PIP joints are swollen and tender symmetrically. She also has small subcutaneous nodules on her elbows. Labs: RF positive, anti-CCP positive, ESR 85 mm/hr, normocytic anaemia. X-ray shows periarticular osteopenia and marginal erosions.
Step 1: Which autoantibody is MORE specific for rheumatoid arthritis and is present in approximately 70% of patients?
✅ Correct — C. Anti-CCP (ACPA) is present in ~70% of RA patients and is highly specific for RA. RF (IgM against the Fc portion of IgG) is positive in ~80% but is less specific as it also occurs in SLE, Sjögren's, and infections. ANA is characteristic of SLE. Anti-dsDNA is specific for SLE.
❌ Incorrect. RF is positive in 80% of RA but lacks specificity (also positive in SLE, Sjögren's, hepatitis). Anti-CCP (ACPA) is positive in ~70% of RA and is much more specific — detecting citrullinated proteins that result from enzymatic modification of self-antigens. ANA → SLE; anti-dsDNA → SLE.
Step 2: The subcutaneous nodules on her elbows are biopsied. Which histological finding is CHARACTERISTIC of rheumatoid nodules?
✅ Correct — B. The classic histology of a rheumatoid nodule is: central fibrinoid necrosis (amorphous eosinophilic material) surrounded by a palisade of macrophages (histiocytes) and lymphocytes, with a peripheral layer of fibrous tissue. They occur at pressure points: elbow, ulnar forearm, occiput, lumbosacral. This is a granulomatous pattern.
❌ Incorrect. Remember: Rheumatoid nodule = central fibrinoid necrosis + palisaded macrophages + peripheral lymphocytes. MSU crystal aggregates with giant cells = tophus (gout). Mucinous cyst lined by synovial cells = ganglion cyst. Pannus = joint finding in RA, not a nodule.
Step 3: Which cytokine, produced by Th17 cells, is responsible for recruiting neutrophils and monocytes into the rheumatoid joint?
✅ Correct — C. IL-17 from Th17 cells recruits neutrophils and monocytes to the inflamed joint. Key cytokine roles in RA: IFN-γ (Th1) → activates macrophages and synoviocytes; TNF-α and IL-1 → proteases destroy cartilage; IL-6 → acute phase response (elevated ESR/CRP); IL-17 (Th17) → neutrophil/monocyte recruitment; RANKL → osteoclast activation → bone erosion.
❌ Incorrect. Cytokine roles in RA: IFN-γ (Th1) activates synovial cells and macrophages; IL-1β and TNF-α drive cartilage destruction via MMPs; IL-17 (Th17) specifically recruits neutrophils and monocytes; RANKL drives osteoclast-mediated bone resorption. Knowing which T cell produces which cytokine and its downstream effect is key for MCQs.
Case 3 · Gout

The Man Who Can't Walk After the Wedding Feast

A 48-year-old male wakes at 3 AM with excruciating pain, redness, and swelling of the right big toe. He attended a wedding the evening before and consumed large amounts of red meat, shellfish, and beer. He has had 2 similar episodes in the past. His GP measures serum urate: 9.2 mg/dL. Joint aspiration shows needle-shaped, negatively birefringent crystals under polarised light. He also has a family history of gout and takes low-dose aspirin.
Step 1: Under polarising microscopy, the crystals from gout appear needle-shaped and negatively birefringent. What is the correct appearance?
✅ Correct — B. MSU crystals (gout) = needle-shaped + negatively birefringent = yellow when parallel to the slow axis of the compensator (blue when perpendicular). CPPD (pseudogout) = rhomboid-shaped + positively birefringent = blue when parallel, yellow when perpendicular. The mnemonic: "Gout = NEGATIVE birefringence; Pseudogout = POSITIVE birefringence."
❌ Incorrect. Crystal comparison: Gout (MSU) = needle-shaped + NEGATIVELY birefringent. Pseudogout (CPPD) = rhomboid-shaped + POSITIVELY birefringent. High-yield exam distinction! Also, negatively birefringent means yellow when crystals are parallel to the slow axis of the polarising compensator.
Step 2: What is the primary cellular mechanism causing severe pain in acute gout?
✅ Correct — C. The key acute gout mechanism: MSU crystals → phagocytosed by macrophages → activate the NLRP3 inflammasome → release IL-1β (primary mediator) + chemokines → neutrophil recruitment → neutrophils phagocytose crystals → lysis → release lysosomal enzymes + more crystals → amplified inflammation and severe pain. This is why IL-1 inhibitors (e.g., anakinra) are effective in gout.
❌ Incorrect. Acute gout pathogenesis is crystal-driven, not autoimmune. The sequence is: MSU crystals → macrophage phagocytosis → NLRP3 inflammasome → IL-1β release → neutrophil chemotaxis → neutrophil crystal phagocytosis → neutrophil lysis → lysosomal enzyme release → tissue injury and pain. Options A and D describe RA mechanisms.
Step 3: The patient develops chronic tophaceous gout. A tophus is biopsied. What is the histological appearance?
✅ Correct — A. A tophus = deposits of MSU crystals surrounded by a granulomatous reaction with foreign body giant cells, palisaded macrophages, and lymphocytes. In fixed tissue, the crystals dissolve leaving amorphous spaces (their shape is preserved only in alcohol-fixed, unstained tissue). Tophi can form in tendons, bursae, cartilage (ear pinna), and kidney (gouty nephropathy).
❌ Incorrect. Tophus = MSU crystal aggregate + granulomatous inflammation (giant cells + palisaded macrophages). Option B = rheumatoid nodule. Option C = RA synovium (pannus precursor). Option D = OA cartilage changes. Each arthritis has a distinctive histological signature — commit these to memory!

Section 08

Key Take-Home Messages

🦴

OA — Wear & Tear, Not Inflammation

OA is fundamentally a mechanical cartilage failure disease. The key sequence is chondrocyte injury → MMP-driven cartilage degradation → eburnation → osteophyte formation. It lacks systemic features and inflammatory markers. Morning stiffness is brief (<30 min). Heberden's nodes (DIP) and Bouchard's nodes (PIP) are osteophytes.

🛡️

RA — Autoimmunity Destroys the Synovium

RA is a systemic autoimmune disease where pannus (hyperplastic synovium) erodes cartilage and bone. RF and anti-CCP antibodies are diagnostic markers. Pannus → fibrous ankylosis → bony ankylosis. The hallmark histology of rheumatoid nodules is fibrinoid necrosis surrounded by palisaded macrophages. Morning stiffness >1 hour is the clinical clue.

💎

Gout — MSU Crystals Drive Inflammasome

Gout is a metabolic disorder where hyperuricaemia leads to MSU crystal deposition, NLRP3 inflammasome activation, and IL-1β-driven neutrophil recruitment. The classic presentation is nocturnal podagra (1st MTP). Tophi = MSU crystals + granulomatous inflammation. MSU crystals are negatively birefringent and needle-shaped on polarising microscopy.

🔗

Seronegative SpA — HLA-B27 is the Key

This group shares RF negativity, HLA-B27 association, sacroiliac involvement, and enthesitis (ligament/tendon insertion pathology). Ankylosing spondylitis causes bamboo spine. Reactive arthritis (Reiter's) follows genitourinary or GI infections and presents as a classic triad: arthritis + urethritis + conjunctivitis.

🔬

Crystal Distinction — Gout vs Pseudogout

Gout = MSU crystals: NEEDLE-shaped, NEGATIVELY birefringent (yellow when parallel). Pseudogout (CPPD) = calcium pyrophosphate crystals: RHOMBOID-shaped, POSITIVELY birefringent (blue when parallel, yellow when perpendicular). Gout affects the 1st MTP joint; pseudogout favours the knee. This distinction is a high-yield MCQ trap.

🏥

Bacterial Arthritis — Organism Matters by Age

Suppurative arthritis requires urgent treatment to prevent permanent joint destruction. Organism prediction by patient profile: neonates → H. influenzae; adults → S. aureus; sickle cell → Salmonella; sexually active women → N. gonorrhoeae. Always aspirate the joint — pus confirms the diagnosis.

Section 09

Exam Focus — Common Traps & Rapid Recall

⚠️ Common Exam Traps

Trap 1: Nodes Confusion

Heberden's nodes (DIP joints) and Bouchard's nodes (PIP joints) are both features of OA — they are osteophytes, NOT rheumatoid nodules. Rheumatoid nodules have fibrinoid necrosis and occur at pressure points, not necessarily joints. They are NOT tophi either.

Trap 2: Crystal Birefringence

Students often swap gout and pseudogout crystals. Remember: gout = NEEDLE + NEGATIVE birefringence; pseudogout = RHOMBOID + POSITIVE birefringence. The common trap is describing gout crystals as rhomboid or saying CPPD is negatively birefringent — both are wrong.

Trap 3: Seronegative = No RF, Not No Antibodies

Seronegative spondyloarthropathies are "seronegative" specifically for Rheumatoid Factor. They are HLA-B27 positive. Students mistakenly think "seronegative" means no autoimmune process at all — the HLA association confirms immune involvement.

Trap 4: Morning Stiffness Duration

The duration of morning stiffness is a critical differentiator in exams. OA: <30 minutes (brief, joint "warms up"). RA: >1 hour (prolonged, systemic). A vignette describing "stiffness relieved after 20 minutes" points to OA, not RA.

Trap 5: OA X-ray vs RA X-ray

OA X-ray: osteophytes, joint space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis, cysts — but NO erosions. RA X-ray: periarticular osteopenia, joint space loss, marginal erosions, ulnar drift — but NO osteophytes. Examiners love asking candidates to distinguish these.

Trap 6: Primary vs Secondary Gout

Primary gout (90%) is mostly idiopathic overproduction or reduced excretion of uric acid. Secondary gout can arise from haematological malignancies (leukemia, lymphoma) due to high cell turnover. A patient with gout and recent chemotherapy → suspect secondary gout from tumour lysis syndrome.
